revenue growth
29% revenue growth mattered because it came from 154 new shops and better sales at existing stores.
  • dutch bros likely made impressive strides in 2025.
  • the top line has been getting a strong boost from new stores (more below), as well as rising sales at established shops, including 5.7% growth in the september quarter.
    most of these gains are being driven by increased transactions, rather than higher prices, indicating that dutch’s beverage-oriented, drivethru shops remain a popular destination with customers.
  • meanwhile, operating margins have faced some pressure of late from elevated coffee costs, higher preopening expenses, and other items, but these headwinds were unlikely to prevent full-year earnings from climbing about 43%, to roughly $0.70 a share. (results were scheduled to be released after we went to press with this report.) new store development continues to ramp up.
    the company was tracking toward opening 160 shops last year and aims to bump this up to 175 in 2026.
  • this year’s activity would expand the store base about 15%, and a mid-teens annual growth rate is apt to persist in subsequent years.
    in all, dutch bros, which opened its 1,000th location just about a year ago, aims to reach 2,000 stores by end of the decade.
  • we look for earnings to climb nearly 30% in 2026.

What could go wrong

the #1 risk is coffee-cost inflation colliding with aggressive store expansion.

∘
med
coffee and input cost pressure
management already flagged higher coffee costs. with a 9.2% net margin, there is not a massive cushion if commodity and labor costs stay elevated.
margin pressure hits an already premium 79.7x earnings multiple fast.
∘
med
new-store execution risk
the plan moves from 160 openings to 175 in 2026. if new shops take longer to mature or cannibalize older ones, revenue growth stops looking clean.
the market is paying for mid-teens store growth. a miss there would hit both the story and the multiple.
∘
med
traffic softening in a weaker consumer tape
dutch bros sells affordable indulgence, but it is still discretionary. if traffic slips while preopening expense stays high, earnings momentum gets pinched.
these risks sit on top of $1.6B in revenue and a chain still proving it can scale nationally without losing unit economics.
the risk stack is simple: a premium valuation assumes the company can keep translating 15% store-base growth into 25%+ sales growth while holding a 9.2% net margin together.
