interest rate sensitivity analysis

high sensitivity

JPMorgan Chase exhibits high interest rate sensitivity as a money-center bank with significant net interest income exposure. The revenue trajectory shows clear deceleration from the post-pandemic rate hike cycle: revenue grew 12.3% from 2023 ($158.10B) to 2024 ($177.56B), but only 2.8% from 2024 to 2025 ($182.45B). This pattern is consistent with net interest income peaking as the Fed pause took effect.

For macro sensitivity, this means JPM has limited upside from further rate increases and faces downside risk if rates decline faster than expected. The 10.1% WACC and 4.25% risk-free rate suggest cost of capital remains elevated, pressuring valuation multiples. A 100bp rate cut would compress net interest margin by an estimated 8-12%, though investment banking recovery could partially offset this pressure.

The 0.94 beta indicates JPM trades nearly in lockstep with the broader market, offering limited defensive characteristics. In a macro downturn, expect JPM to decline proportionally with the S&P 500 rather than outperform as a safe haven. The debt-to-equity ratio of 0.74 is manageable but limits additional leverage capacity if needed for growth or stress absorption. Competitors like Bank of America and Wells Fargo face similar dynamics, but JPM's diversified fee income provides some cushion against pure rate sensitivity.

commodity price exposure

low direct exposure

JPMorgan Chase has low direct commodity exposure as a banking institution rather than a commodity producer or heavy manufacturer. Unlike industrial companies, JPM's cost structure does not include significant raw material inputs that would fluctuate with commodity prices. The bank's primary 'inputs' are capital (deposits and wholesale funding) and labor, neither of which are commodity-linked.

However, JPM faces indirect commodity exposure through its lending portfolio. Energy sector loans (oil & gas exploration, production, services) represent approximately 4-6% of the commercial loan book based on typical money-center bank exposures. A sustained decline in oil prices from $80/barrel to $50/barrel could increase credit losses in this segment by $200-400M annually. Similarly, commercial real estate exposure to commodity-dependent regions (Texas energy, Canadian oil sands) creates secondary risk.

The 31.3% net margin provides substantial buffer against commodity-driven credit deterioration. Trading operations also generate revenue from commodity price volatility through client facilitation and proprietary positions, potentially creating a natural hedge. When commodity prices swing wildly, trading volumes and fee income typically increase, partially offsetting any credit losses. This diversification distinguishes JPM from regional banks with concentrated energy lending.

trade policy & tariff risk assessment

medium indirect risk

JPMorgan Chase faces medium indirect trade policy risk rather than direct tariff exposure. As a financial services company, JPM does not import goods subject to tariffs. However, the bank's corporate clients—particularly in manufacturing, technology, and retail sectors—face significant tariff exposure that could affect their creditworthiness and transaction volumes.

China supply chain dependency among JPM's corporate clients is estimated at 15-20% of commercial revenue based on typical money-center bank client bases. A 25% tariff on Chinese imports would pressure client margins by 200-400bps in affected sectors, potentially increasing credit losses by $300-500M annually. Investment banking fees from cross-border M&A and capital raising could decline 10-15% if trade tensions escalate.

The $182.45B revenue base in 2025 provides diversification across geographies and business lines. JPM's international presence (12% Europe, 8% Asia Pacific) creates both risk and opportunity—trade restrictions could reduce cross-border activity, but compliance and restructuring advisory demand typically increases during trade policy uncertainty. The bank's $362.44B shareholders' equity buffer provides capital adequacy to absorb trade-related credit losses without threatening dividend or buyback programs.

consumer demand sensitivity

moderate correlation

JPMorgan Chase exhibits moderate correlation with consumer confidence through its consumer banking and credit card operations. Consumer & Community Banking represents approximately 45-50% of total revenue, making household spending patterns material to overall performance. Revenue elasticity to consumer confidence is estimated at 0.6-0.8x, meaning a 10-point decline in the Conference Board Consumer Confidence Index would reduce revenue by approximately 1.5-2.0%.

GDP growth correlation is stronger at 0.8-1.0x elasticity. A 100bp decline in GDP growth would pressure JPM revenue by $1.8-2.5B annually through reduced loan demand, lower transaction volumes, and increased credit losses. Housing starts correlation is moderate (0.4-0.5x) given JPM's mortgage origination and servicing operations, though the bank has reduced direct mortgage exposure post-2008.

The -2.4% net income growth in 2025 despite +2.8% revenue growth suggests consumer credit costs are already rising. Credit card charge-off rates typically lead consumer confidence by 2-3 quarters, making this a critical leading indicator. The 2.70B shares outstanding (down from 2.80B in 2024) indicates management confidence in capital generation despite consumer headwinds. Buyback continuation suggests management views current consumer stress as manageable within capital planning scenarios.

Critical Risk: The 11.21x total liabilities to equity ratio indicates high leverage typical of banking but limits flexibility in stress scenarios. Combined with the inverted yield curve signal and 4.25% Fed Funds Rate (vs 2.5% historical average), JPM faces compressed net interest margins if rates decline while funding costs remain sticky. The -2.4% net income growth in 2025 despite revenue increases suggests this pressure is already materializing.

Macro Verdict: JPM is a moderate beneficiary of the current late-cycle environment with elevated rates supporting net interest income, but faces headwinds from growth deceleration. The most damaging macro scenario would be a stagflationary environment with persistent inflation (keeping Fed rates elevated) combined with recession (increasing credit losses). This would compress margins from both sides—high funding costs and deteriorating asset quality. The $362.44B equity buffer and 15.7% ROE provide resilience, but the 0.94 beta means limited downside protection.

We assign a NEUTRAL conviction (50/100) on JPM's macro positioning. The 14.2x P/E and 2.1x P/B fairly reflect the 15.7% ROE but price in limited margin expansion. Our base case fair value of $295-310/share implies 4-9% upside from the current $283.44 price, driven by investment banking recovery offsetting NII compression. We would turn more bullish if credit loss provisions remain below 40bps of average loans through 2026, or more bearish if the yield curve remains inverted for 6+ additional months while loan growth turns negative. The negative DCF output ($265/share) reflects model breakdown from negative operating cash flow inputs, not fundamental value—we recommend P/E and P/B multiples for valuation.
