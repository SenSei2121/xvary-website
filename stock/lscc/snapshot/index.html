What they do
Lattice sells small programmable chips and the software around them that help devices connect, control, and secure themselves.
Lattice wins by being the chip you design around, then hate to replace. Programmable logic devices (chips you can reprogram after manufacturing, so what: customers can tweak products without redesigning the whole board) create sticky design wins, and gross margin was 68.1% in the latest filing. The company also spent 24.8% of 2025 sales on R&D, so your risk is not weak engineering effort, it is whether demand catches up to the stock price.

the number that mattered
The key number was 24% revenue growth in the December period, because that says the recovery is real enough to show up in sales, not just cost control.
  • lattice semiconductor remains in recovery mode.
  • revenues for the december period were up 9% sequentially and 24% vs. prior year, to $145.8 million, the company’s best performance in two years.
  • the improvement was powered by the communications and computing markets, where sales jumped nearly 60% from the prior-year period, easily offsetting continued weakness in industrial and automotive markets (down 10%).
    meanwhile, adjusted earnings reached $0.32 a share, more than double the 2024 comparable period, which included a $0.05 charge related to test and assembly operations.
  • the turnaround should gain momentum in 2026.
  • the company’s march-quarter outlook calls for vs. prior year sales and earnings growth of 31%–43% and 55%-73%, respectively, well ahead of wall street’s earlier expectations.
    the rest of the year appears promising, too, prompting us to bump up our 2026 top- and bottom-line estimates by $60 million and $0.15 a share. management emphasizes that lattice’s field-programmable gate arrays (fpgas), though not at the center of the artificial intelligence (ai) revolution, play a myriad of supporting roles. demand for these products should continue to benefit from rising spending on data centers, as well as emerging efforts to incorporate ai into cameras, radar, and other devices that interact with the real world. the resulting growth will likely be most noticeable in communications and computing markets, though revenues at industrial and automotive customers should also begin to bounce back, particularly now that customers seem to have finished working through excess inventories.
