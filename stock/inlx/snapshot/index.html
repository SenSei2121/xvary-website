What could go wrong

the #1 risk is failing to turn a 66.5% gross margin and $12.1M software revenue base into profit before the market loses patience.

∘
med
the revenue base is small enough that fixed costs still win
INLX produced $18M of revenue and still lost $1.9M. That's the core problem. At this size, one hiring push, one slower quarter, or one stalled sales cycle can wipe out the benefit of a decent gross margin.
if revenue stays around $18M and net margin stays below zero, the valuation starts to look less like upside and more like an accurate read on a subscale software company.
∘
med
document management is crowded and scale usually wins
The current page already points to Box as a larger rival. That's enough to make the point. INLX operates in categories where bigger vendors have broader sales reach and more room to compete on price.
if pricing power stays weak, a 66.5% gross margin does not expand much from here and the path to profit gets longer.
∘
med
the CEO change buys time, not results
Alison Forsythe took over on Feb 17, 2026. Leadership changes can sharpen focus. They can also reset targets, delay decisions, and give investors a grace period that the numbers do not earn on their own.
you are asking a new CEO to fix a business that lost $1.9M last year. that is an operating job, not a messaging job.
∘
med
one-third of revenue is still the slower, weaker piece
Professional services produced $5.9M, or 33% of revenue, and declined 2% from last year. That matters because services still make up one-third of the business today.
if that segment keeps shrinking, software growth has to outrun it just to keep total revenue moving. $5.9M of softer revenue is not background noise in an $18M company.
Software and subscriptions generated $12.1M, yet the company still lost $1.9M. That exposes the whole investment case to one question: can management scale the good revenue faster than costs and services drag absorb it.
