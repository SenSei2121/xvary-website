What they do
TechPrecision makes custom metal parts and assemblies for defense, aerospace, nuclear, and medical equipment.
You are not buying a broad vendor list. You are buying access to a shop that makes parts for ships, submarines, helicopters, and nuclear plants. Ten customers made 93% of FY2024 revenue, and the largest one made 28%. That is customer concentration, which means a few buyers hold your revenue by the throat.

What could go wrong

TechPrecision does not need a catastrophe to hurt you. It needs one more quarter where losses stay heavy, cash stays near $70k, and project timing works against a company this small.

∘
med
chronic losses keep eating the clock
TPCS lost $7.04M over the last fiscal year and another $1.8M in Q3 2026. These are not isolated misses. They are recurring operating outcomes.
On a $34M revenue base, losses of that size shorten the runway quickly and make every next quarter matter more.
∘
med
thin liquidity turns timing into risk
Cash of $70k is tiny for an operating manufacturer. One delayed receivable, one cost overrun, or one messy project can matter immediately.
When liquidity is this tight, management has less time and fewer good options if the next quarter disappoints.
∘
med
too much debt for a 1.9% margin business
Debt-to-equity is 131.7% and operating margin is just 1.9%. Debt is easier to carry when margins are strong. These are not.
If profitability does not improve, the capital structure stops being background and becomes the central problem.
∘
med
small-company concentration cuts both ways
Precision Fabrication is 68% of revenue and Machined Systems is 32%. On a $34M base, a few delayed programs can move the whole result.
Project concentration means reported revenue and margin can swing harder than you would expect from the headline industry label.
A company doing $34M in revenue with a $7.04M annual loss, $70k cash, and 131.7% debt-to-equity does not need a disaster to hurt you. It just needs another weak quarter.
