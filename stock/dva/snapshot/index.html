the number that mattered
The $3.40 print beat $2.99 by 13.71%, and that is the number that told the market the quarter was stronger than expected.
  • davita reported strong fourth-quarter results.
  • consolidated revenues of $3.62 billion represented a 10% increase over the prior-year period tally of $3.29 billion, with a 0.9% increase in u.s. treatment volumes per day.
    an increase in average reimbursement rates, along with the seasonal benefit from flu vaccines, and overall improved product mix, drove the revenue gain. revenue per treatment increased $12.01 sequentially, with higher patient responsibility for copays and deductibles contributing as well. management proved increasingly aggressive with its stock-repurchasing activity during the december interim, buying back 2.7 million shares for $331 million, at an average price of $122.78 per share. this contributed further to driving adjusted diluted earnings per share of $3.40, handily exceeding our forecast of $2.99, as well as the wall street consensus.
  • leadership also provided an encouraging outlook for 2026, projecting adjusted earnings per share to range between $13.60 and $15.00, reflecting 33% growth at the midpoint when compared to the full-year 2025 figure of $10.78.
  • long term, we are modeling 7%–11% annual adjusted share-earnings gains, slightly below projection.
    as of december 31st, davita provided dialysis services to approximately 295,000 patients at 3,242 outpatient dialysis centers.
  • davita has announced a strategic investment into elara caring, an acute home healthcare hospice provider.
    the approximate $200 million minority investment, made alongside a majority investment from ares private equity funds, is targeted for a mid-year closing.

What could go wrong

the #1 risk is dialysis reimbursement pressure from medicare and private insurers. davita can operate 3,242 centers efficiently, but it cannot unilaterally raise the price of a regulated treatment.

∘
med
reimbursement rate cuts
this business lives on what payors decide a dialysis treatment is worth. if government or commercial reimbursement tightens, revenue and margin feel it immediately.
a 5% rate cut would pressure roughly $680M of annual revenue against a business that only posts a 7.3% net margin.
∘
med
slow treatment-volume growth
u.s. treatment volumes per day grew 0.9% last quarter. that's positive, but it leaves very little cushion if patient growth stalls or treatment frequency softens.
the snapshot does not break out enough per-treatment detail to size the revenue hit precisely, but with only 0.9% volume growth, there is not much buffer.
∘
med
balance sheet constraint
$10.2B in long-term debt equals 55% of capital. that does not make the equity broken, but it does mean policy pressure and operating slips compound faster.
when a company with an $8B market cap carries $10.2B in long-term debt, room for error is finite.
reimbursement pressure reaches essentially all of the $13.6B revenue base, and the $10.2B debt load means even a steady operator does not get unlimited second chances.
