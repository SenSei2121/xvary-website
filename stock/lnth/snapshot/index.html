What they do
Lantheus makes scans and cancer-targeting drugs that help doctors find disease, track it, and sometimes treat it.
This business wins because hospitals do not casually swap out imaging agents once workflows are built. Switching costs (changing systems) → operational hassle → you keep buying from the vendor that already works. Lantheus turned that stickiness into a 20.2% operating margin and 21.5% return on capital, which is well above what most healthcare suppliers manage.

What could go wrong

the #1 risk here is PYLARIFY concentration. When one franchise carries the narrative, any slowdown starts to hit the stock before it fully hits the income statement.

∘
med
PYLARIFY concentration
This page does not break out product mix, but the narrative already does. Investors are mostly paying attention to PYLARIFY. If that franchise loses momentum, the rest of the portfolio has to work harder just to keep the multiple from shrinking.
With the stock at 12.2x trailing earnings, you are not paying for perfection. You are still exposed to one product doing too much of the emotional work.
∘
med
procedure volume softness
Diagnostic imaging and radiotherapeutics are tied to real-world procedure flow. If scan volumes or treatment activity cools, a business with 34.0% operating margin can feel that quickly.
High-margin businesses look great on the way up. They can also show downside faster when throughput slows.
∘
med
the low multiple may be a message
A 36% growth company trading at 12.2x earnings can be mispriced. It can also be the market telling you current profits are not trusted. The 20/100 earnings predictability score does not exactly calm that argument.
If revenue falls short of the $2B FY2026 estimate while margins slip from 34.0%, the stock can get hit by weaker earnings and a lower multiple at the same time.
A business doing $1.5B in revenue with a 24.5% net margin has real earning power. The risk is that the market only fully believes it as long as the lead franchise keeps delivering.
