interest rate sensitivity analysis

rate risk

Pfizer's interest rate sensitivity is moderated by its defensive beta of 0.48, which indicates low correlation to broader market macro shocks. However, the company faces meaningful refinancing risk given Total Liabilities of $121.39B against Cash & Equivalents of only $1.14B. This liquidity mismatch creates vulnerability if rates remain elevated during debt rollover periods.

The WACC is modeled at 6.1% in our base case, benefiting from the low beta and current debt structure. However, the Market Calibration shows an Implied WACC of 8.7%, suggesting investors demand a significantly higher risk premium than our model assumes. A 100 basis point increase in rates would pressure the discount rate, potentially reducing the Base Scenario fair value from $57.90 toward the $45-50 range depending on terminal growth assumptions.

The Debt to Equity ratio of 0.71 (book value) indicates moderate leverage, but the Total Liabilities to Equity ratio of 1.4x reveals broader obligation exposure. With the Risk-Free Rate at 4.25% in our WACC calculation, any sustained increase in Treasury yields would directly impact the Cost of Equity, currently calculated at 6.9%. The company's FCF Yield of 6.0% provides some cushion, exceeding the risk-free rate and supporting dividend sustainability even in a higher-rate environment.

Critical data gaps limit precise sensitivity modeling: the debt maturity schedule is not available in the current EDGAR filings (Long-Term Debt data stops at 2020), and the floating versus fixed debt mix is unknown. These gaps prevent calculation of immediate refinancing walls and exact duration exposure. Management should disclose upcoming maturities to allow investors to assess rollover risk more accurately.

commodity input cost exposure

input costs

Pfizer's commodity exposure is relatively limited compared to manufacturing-heavy industries, given the pharmaceutical business model's reliance on intellectual property rather than raw material inputs. The Gross Margin of 74.3% provides a substantial buffer against inflationary pressure on cost of goods sold. COGS for FY2025 totaled $16.07B on Revenue of $62.58B, representing approximately 25.7% of revenue.

Key input commodities for pharmaceutical manufacturing include active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), chemical precursors, and packaging materials. While specific commodity hedging programs are not disclosed in the available EDGAR filings, the high gross margin suggests the company has significant pass-through ability to customers. Unlike commodity producers, Pfizer can typically adjust pricing on patented drugs without immediate competitive pressure, providing a natural hedge against input cost inflation.

The SG&A expense ratio of 22.0% of revenue represents a larger operational leverage risk than commodity costs. Labor cost inflation within SG&A (sales, marketing, R&D personnel) could expand faster than the -1.6% revenue growth environment, compressing the Net Margin of 12.4%. This operational leverage is a double-edged sword: in a growth environment, margins expand rapidly, but during contraction, fixed cost structures pressure profitability.

Historical impact of commodity price swings on margins appears muted based on the stable gross margin profile. The FCF Margin of 14.5% and FCF Yield of 6.0% suggest the company maintains pricing power sufficient to offset input cost pressures. However, the EDGAR filings does not provide specific quantification of inflation impact on COGS versus SG&A separately, limiting precise sensitivity analysis. Management commentary on input cost trends would enhance investor understanding of this risk factor.

trade policy and tariff risk assessment

policy risk

Pfizer faces medium-level trade policy risk given its global supply chain and multinational revenue base. The pharmaceutical industry is subject to various regulatory regimes, import/export controls, and potential tariff actions that could impact manufacturing costs and market access. While specific tariff exposure by product and region is not quantified in the available EDGAR filings, the company's global footprint creates inherent vulnerability to trade policy shifts.

China supply chain dependency is a critical consideration for pharmaceutical manufacturers, as many active pharmaceutical ingredients are sourced from Asian suppliers. The evidence confirms Pfizer discusses foreign currency risk in SEC filings, indicating significant international operations, but specific China dependency percentages are not provided. A sustained deterioration in US-China trade relations could disrupt supply chains and increase COGS, pressuring the 74.3% Gross Margin.

Potential tariff scenarios could impact margins through multiple channels: direct tariffs on imported ingredients would increase COGS, retaliatory tariffs could limit market access in key growth regions, and regulatory barriers could delay product approvals. The 12.4% Net Margin provides some buffer, but sustained trade policy headwinds could compress this toward the 10-11% range if pass-through to customers is limited by pricing regulations or competitive dynamics.

The Revenue Growth YoY of -1.6% suggests the company is already facing headwinds, which could be exacerbated by adverse trade policy developments. However, the defensive nature of pharmaceutical demand (inelastic consumption) provides some protection against trade-driven economic slowdowns. Investors should monitor management commentary on supply chain diversification efforts and any disclosure of geographic manufacturing footprint shifts in response to trade policy uncertainty.

macroeconomic demand sensitivity

demand elasticity

Pfizer's demand profile exhibits low correlation with consumer confidence and traditional economic cycle indicators, consistent with the defensive characteristics of the pharmaceutical sector. The Beta of 0.48 confirms low volatility relative to the broader market, indicating that company-specific factors outweigh general macroeconomic conditions in driving stock performance. This defensive positioning is valuable during economic contractions when cyclical sectors face significant demand destruction.

Revenue elasticity to GDP growth is estimated to be low (approximately 0.2-0.3x), meaning a 1% decline in GDP would impact Pfizer revenue by only 0.2-0.3%. This inelastic demand stems from the essential nature of pharmaceutical products: patients require medications regardless of economic conditions, and insurance coverage (government and private) buffers direct consumer spending pressure. The $62.58B FY2025 revenue base demonstrates this stability despite the -1.6% YoY contraction, which appears driven by portfolio maturation rather than macro demand weakness.

Housing starts and consumer confidence indices have minimal direct impact on Pfizer's business model. However, indirect effects exist through employment-linked health insurance coverage: during severe recessions, job losses could reduce insured populations, potentially impacting prescription volumes in certain therapeutic areas. The US healthcare system's structure (Medicare, Medicaid, employer-sponsored insurance) provides significant insulation from these dynamics compared to consumer discretionary sectors.

The Implied Growth Rate of -12.3% from market calibration suggests investors are pricing in fundamental deterioration beyond normal macro cycles. This disconnect between defensive beta and negative growth expectations highlights company-specific execution risk (patent cliffs, pipeline delivery) rather than macro demand sensitivity. For portfolio construction, Pfizer serves as a macro hedge: it should outperform during economic downturns while underperforming during strong expansion periods when cyclical sectors rally.
