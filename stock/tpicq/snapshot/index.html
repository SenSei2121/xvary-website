What they do
TPI makes giant composite wind blades and the molds and parts needed to build them.
If your customer needs a 200-foot wind blade, you do not ship that like a box of screws. TPI has 7 blade plants across the U.S., China, and Mexico, plus 11,700 employees building the stuff close to where turbines get assembled. Scale moat (hard-to-copy factory network) → a spread-out manufacturing footprint → so what: you cannot replace that overnight when blades are huge and logistics are brutal.

What could go wrong

the top risk is equity wipeout in Chapter 11. This is not a normal downside case. It is the default outcome common shareholders have to disprove.

!
high
Chapter 11 reorganization
The company and affiliates filed for Chapter 11 on Aug. 11, 2025.
Common stock typically sits behind secured lenders, unsecured creditors, and other claims. If the estate is not worth more than those claims, equity gets nothing.
!
high
core asset sale at $20M
On Mar. 6, 2026, TPI signed an agreement to sell its wind blade business to ECP Blade Holdings LLC for $20M.
That sale transfers the operating business that once produced $1.3B in annual revenue. If proceeds only cover part of the creditor stack, shareholders are holding the shell.
∘
med
going concern warning
The November 2025 10-Q stated that substantial doubt exists about the company's ability to continue as a going concern.
In plain English: management itself disclosed that the business could not keep operating normally without restructuring support.
∘
med
thin operating economics before bankruptcy
Gross margin was 11.9%, operating margin was 1.8%, and net profit margin was -15.3%.
Those numbers say the operating model was already under strain before the court process. A business this thin has very little rescue margin.
A $20M sale price against a business that last reported $1.3B in revenue is the clearest signal on this page. It quantifies how little room common equity may have after higher-priority claims are paid.
