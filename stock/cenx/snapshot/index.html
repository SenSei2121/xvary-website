What they do
Century makes primary aluminum, plus the carbon inputs smelters need, from plants in the U.S., Iceland, the Netherlands, and China.
Aluminum is a scale and power-cost business. Century has four smelters with 1,016,000 tonnes of annual capacity, plus its own carbon anode assets, so you are not relying on outside suppliers for every critical input. That matters because return on capital is 24.5%, while operating margin is only 6.3%—thin margins punish weaker operators fast.

the number that mattered
Revenue jumping 200% vs. prior year matters most because this company lives and dies by aluminum pricing and shipment economics, not by tidy recurring sales.
  • we have cut our 2025 earnings estimate for century aluminum by $0.20, to $2.05 a share.
    indeed, the company reported adjusted third-quarter profits of $0.56 a share, well below both our estimate and the consensus.
  • this marks the third-consecutive quarter where results were below wall street expectations.
  • revenues of $632 million were essentially flat with the second-quarter tally, but were up 17% vs. prior year.
    strong pricing, driven by a large jump in the midwest premium was enough to offset production headwinds that plagued the company throughout the period.
  • shipments declined 7% sequentially.
    the grundartangi smelter in iceland suffered two transformer failures that put a kink into its production. line 2 at the plant remains down and it could take 11 to 12 months for replacement transformers to be installed. in the meantime, the company is looking into repairing the damaged transformers to use while waiting for the new ones to arrive. meanwhile, the mount holly facility in south carolina experienced a period of operational instability, which hurt production to the tune of 4,000 tonnes. the problems were resolved by mid-october, however, and the plant is back to operating at normal production levels.
  • we have increased our 2026 earnings estimate by $0.60, to $3.30.
    demand for aluminum remains strong, particularly in the u.s. and europe (century’s key markets) and supply remains tight, thanks to tariffs that make importing the metal expensive. worldwide production is also being hamstrung as china (the world’s largest producer) is nearing its annual production cap of 45 million tons, and plans to build new smelters in indonesia face setbacks due to high energy costs and regulatory hurdles.
