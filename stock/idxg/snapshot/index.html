What they do
Interpace sells genetic tests that help doctors decide whether a thyroid or pancreatic nodule looks dangerous.
Doctors do not buy vibes. They buy test results that change treatment. Interpace turned that into a 61.3% gross margin (gross margin → money left after paying to run each test → so what: the test menu has pricing power). With 111 employees and just $1 million of long-term debt, you are looking at a small lab that does not need a giant empire to matter.

What could go wrong

the #1 risk is single-test dependence in thyroid diagnostics. when 100% of a $40M revenue target rides on one category, you do not have diversification. you have concentration.

∘
med
thyroid concentration
The current growth case points 100% of the guided $40M 2026 revenue to thyroid testing. If ordering volume weakens or a competing assay wins share, the entire model feels it.
Impact: this puts effectively all forward revenue concentration in one clinical lane.
∘
med
reimbursement pressure
PancraGEN was stopped after CMS reimbursement changes. That is not ancient history. It is a live example of how payor policy can damage test economics in this business.
Impact: pricing or coverage changes can hit both revenue and confidence at the same time.
∘
med
thin cash and balance sheet flexibility
The company shows $1.42M of cash, a C balance sheet grade, and price stability of just 5/100. Small companies can survive that. They do not get to ignore it.
Impact: any stumble can force financing, delay strategy, or weaken listing ambitions.
A reimbursement setback on the thyroid business would pressure the revenue base that management wants to scale to $40M, while a thin cash position leaves little room to absorb mistakes.
