  • peloton interactive continues to face a challenging operating environment.
  • indeed, since the company posted record sales during the pandemic, its business has been steadily declining.
    during that period, peloton took advantage of the closure of in-person gyms to expand its customer base and also made heavy investments in increasing capacity. those plans seemed to backfire when pandemic-era restrictions ended and many exercise enthusiasts sought other options to work out.
  • not only did gyms re-open, but a slew of other online fitness platforms entered the fray.
  • now peloton is fighting to keep what's left of its dwindling market share.
    the company recently launched its cross training series that features updated versions of its treadmills, stationary bikes, and rowing machines along with other enhancements.
  • still, the new series comes with increased prices for equipment and membership fees.
    the next few quarters should provide a glimpse of how receptive customers will be of the new offerings.

the main risk is simple: Peloton is trying to finish a consumer turnaround while carrying $1.3B in long-term debt and selling products people can postpone.
