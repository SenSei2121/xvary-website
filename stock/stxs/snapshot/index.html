What they do
It sells robotic systems that guide catheter tips through blood vessels with magnets instead of a doctor’s hand.
Manual catheters move from the handle. Stereotaxis moves the tip with magnetic fields, so your doctor is steering the wire without touching it. That matters because the company says direct tip control can improve precision, stability, reach, and safety, and it did that with 139 employees and about 1.5% market share.

What could go wrong

the main risk is simple: STXS stays a real product with a small business attached to it. At roughly 1.5% share and $32.4M in revenue, this company does not have much room for "almost" scaling.

∘
med
share gains do not arrive
Stereotaxis still sits around ~1.5% market share in surgical robotics. That leaves the company relying on hospitals to choose a niche magnetic-navigation workflow over bigger, better-known alternatives.
At $32.4M in annual revenue, there is not enough scale to shrug off stalled adoption.
∘
med
profitability keeps moving out
Analysts still expect a -$0.30 EPS result, and management is only targeting positive cash flow from operations in 2026. A 53.7% gross margin helps, but it does not solve an expense base spread across a very small company.
If losses persist while revenue stays below $40M+, dilution or retrenchment get easier to imagine.
∘
med
the 2025 inventory build does not convert into growth
Management put $5.6M into working capital in 2025 while building manufacturing and commercial capability. That only looks smart if it supports real volume next.
If 2026 does not show revenue follow-through, the investment starts looking like preparation without payoff.
With $32.4M in 2025 revenue, ~1.5% share, and a $40M+ target for 2026, the gap between interesting technology and durable business is still wide enough to matter.
