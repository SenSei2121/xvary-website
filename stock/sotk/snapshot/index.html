What they do
Sono-Tek sells ultrasonic machines that spray ultra-thin coatings onto medical, energy, and electronics products.
Sono-Tek wins by getting built into your production line, not added as a nice-to-have tool. Once your process is tuned around its ultrasonic coating systems, swapping vendors means reworking yield, speed, and quality checks across the line. What could go wrong

The central risk is simple: Sono-Tek is too small for order slippage to stay local. When one $820K deal equals roughly 4% of annual revenue and the latest quarter only produced $5.0M, you do not need a crisis to get a bad print. You just need timing to turn against you.

∘
med
customer concentration and uneven orders
A single $820K medical device order equals about 4% of annual revenue. This is a project business. If one customer delays spending, the income statement feels it right away.
Impact: one delayed or lost order can swing a quarter from stable to ugly.
∘
med
thin profitability and a C++ balance sheet
The company stayed profitable with a 7.7% net margin last quarter, but that is not a thick buffer. Add a C++ balance sheet grade and you get a setup with limited room for operational mistakes.
Impact: a few soft quarters would pressure both sentiment and flexibility.
∘
med
electronics and clean-energy softness
Electronics & Semiconductors fell 2% last year, and management commentary pointed to slower clean-energy demand. Those two buckets represent 60% of revenue combined.
Impact: if both stay soft, medical has to carry too much of the growth burden.
∘
med
execution load is high for a $66M company
Sono-Tek disclosed 39 risk factors. That does not mean disaster is imminent. It does mean a very small public company has a lot of ways to get knocked off pace.
Impact: small operating misses become public-company problems fast.
Put together, the risks all point to the same thing: the company is real, but the margin for error is small because the revenue base is small.
