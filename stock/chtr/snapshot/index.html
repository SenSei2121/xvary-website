What they do
Charter sells your home internet, cable TV, mobile plans, phone service, and local ads under the Spectrum brand.
Charter wins because your internet bill is sticky and switching is annoying. It had 29.7 million internet customers at 12/31/25, versus 12.6 million pay-TV and 6.0 million voice customers. That local network footprint gives it repeat monthly revenue, even while weaker products shrink.

What just happened
eps beat · revenue light
Charter's last reported quarter was mixed: diluted EPS at $10.34 beat a street consensus near $10.01, while Q4 revenue of $13.6B missed sales estimates.
The EPS beat was roughly 3% versus that consensus—not the whole story. Revenue fell 2.3% vs. prior year in the quarter, and full-year revenue slipped 0.6% to $54.8B while broadband losses kept piling up.

the number that mattered
The number that mattered was still broadband: an EPS beat did not offset another quarter of internet subscriber losses and a revenue line that missed expectations.
  • indeed, service disconnects totaled 119,000 in the period, marking the ninth-straight quarter of broadband-subscriber losses for the connecticut-based cable service provider.
  • the losses are adding up, even after the company both lowered the price of its service plans across the board in september, 2024 and continues to make use of pricelocks and other retention tools.
  • the market backdrop for charter and its peers may remain challenging this year, as well.
  • underpinning our pessimism is what we expect will be a further down-market move by generally lowerincome households towards both fairly inexpensive fixed-wireless broadband offerings from the likes of t-mobile and basic internet access through unlimited cellphone plans.
    a silver lining may be less pushback against charter’s proposed merger with privately held cox communications.
  • ahead of the federal communications commission’s decision on whether to allow the $34.5 billion deal, various consumer groups have recently argued that the combination is anti-competitive and that it will lead to higher prices for internet service.
    that said, market inroads by t-mobile and others, along with the cable sector’s ongoing struggles, suggest to us that a charter-cox combo won’t have undue influence over key industry dynamics.

the main risk here is simple: charter already posted a 0.6% annual revenue decline, so even modest subscriber pressure hits a business with very little growth cushion.
