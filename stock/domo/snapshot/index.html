What could go wrong

the central risk is simple: Domo has software-like margins without software-like growth. If that gap stays open, the low valuation stops looking unfair and starts looking accurate.

∘
med
growth never re-accelerates
Subscription revenue grew 1% to $310M. That is almost the entire business. If the core stays stuck around this pace, Domo remains a 74.4% gross-margin software company without the scale investors usually pay for.
Impact: the market keeps valuing the company below its $317M annual revenue because slow growth turns good software economics into a waiting room.
∘
med
profitability fades after one quarter
Q4 delivered the first quarterly non-GAAP operating profit with margin above 10%, but analysts still expect full-year EPS of -$2.13. That gap is your warning label. One quarter can signal a turn. It can also flatter a cost cut.
Impact: if margins slip back below zero, the recent quarter stops looking like an inflection and starts looking like a cameo.
∘
med
debt limits the room for error
Long-term debt is $131M, or 40% of capital, against a $197M market cap. For a business with a 5 / 100 price stability score, that matters more than it would at a larger scale.
Impact: a few weak quarters do not just hurt sentiment. They tighten the balance-sheet story too.
∘
med
the product story stays hard to separate
Domo talks about apps, AI, and platform breadth, but this snapshot only shows two revenue buckets: $310M of subscription revenue and $7M of services. When disclosure is this thin, you have fewer clean signals on what is actually driving demand.
Impact: you are forced to judge the business mostly through top-line growth, billings, and margin. If those wobble, there is not much segment detail to soften the blow.
A software company with $317M in revenue, 1% growth, and $131M in long-term debt does not get many free mistakes.
