What could go wrong

NHTC's risk stack is unusually concentrated: one shrinking revenue line, one loud dividend, and one recent buyback that was large relative to the size of the company.

∘
med
The core business keeps shrinking
Full-year revenue fell 7.4% to $39.8M, and Q4 came in at $9.7M, down 10% from a year ago. Both reported segments declined, so there is no healthy division carrying the rest.
If that trend continues, the investment case turns into balance-sheet triage and dividend math. That is not a growth story. It is a survival story.
∘
med
The dividend starts competing with the business
The company reported a $588k quarterly loss and still paid a $0.10 quarterly dividend. The payout runs around $1M per quarter, which is real cash for a company this small.
If losses persist, management eventually chooses between keeping the yield and keeping more capital inside the business. High yields usually look best right before that conversation gets uncomfortable.
∘
med
High gross margin is masking weak operating leverage
Gross margin was 73.8% for the year and 72.9% in the latest quarter. Those are healthy product economics. They did not translate into healthy profits because the sales base is too small and moving the wrong way.
A few more points of margin pressure would matter, but the quieter risk is that even stable margin may not be enough if revenue keeps sliding.
∘
med
Capital allocation gets harder after the $5.9M repurchase
NHTC spent $5.9M to buy back 2.94M shares from the George K. Broady family on Feb. 17, 2026. For a company with a roughly $26M market cap, that is a balance-sheet-level decision.
If operating results do not improve, that repurchase will read less like opportunism and more like cash that could have buffered the downturn.
A $39.8M revenue business with a quarterly loss and a roughly $1M quarterly dividend does not have much room for wishful thinking.
