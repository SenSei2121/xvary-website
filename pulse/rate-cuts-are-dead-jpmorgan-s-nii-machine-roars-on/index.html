Rate Cuts Are Dead: JPMorgan's NII Machine Roars On

Wall Street begs for easing while JPMorgan quietly banks record interest income in a higher-for-longer world that crushes the consensus.

xvary research desk · april 1, 2026 · ~3 min read

The conventional wisdom on Wall Street right now is pathetic: price in two, maybe three Fed rate cuts for 2026 and watch bank margins collapse. Wrong. As rate cut expectations get brutally priced out amid sticky inflation and resilient growth, JPMorgan Chase is positioned to deliver its upgraded $104.5 billion net interest income target with room to spare. This isn't a plateau—it's a fortress built on deposit stability and loan demand that the dovish herd completely missed.

JPMorgan's own economists have been the most hawkish voice on the Street, forecasting zero rate cuts in 2026 and even a 25 basis point hike in 2027. The Fed funds rate stays locked between 3.50%-3.75%, with the 10-year Treasury yield hovering near 4.3% as of early April. Markets that once bet on aggressive easing now price in little to no relief this year. For a bank like JPMorgan, whose massive balance sheet thrives when short-term rates don't crater, this is pure oxygen.

Data doesn't lie. JPMorgan guided full-year 2026 firmwide NII to approximately $104.5 billion in February, up from earlier $103 billion forecasts and beating consensus around $102.9 billion. Core NII excluding markets sits at roughly $95 billion—flat to slightly up from 2025 levels despite an anticipated $2 billion rate headwind being more than offset by revolving card balances, institutional loan growth, and modest deposit expansion. That's not survival; that's dominance when peers are scrambling.

Recall the 2025 baseline: JPMorgan posted $95.9 billion in NII. The 2026 jump to $104.5 billion represents over 9% growth in a supposed "normalization" environment. Credit card net charge-offs are projected at a manageable 3.4%, while expenses tick to $105 billion on continued tech and talent investment. ROTCE targets hold steady at 17-18%. This is brutal efficiency in action—no hedging, no excuses.

The contrarian truth is that higher rates for longer aren't a bug for money-center banks; they're the feature. JPMorgan's deposit franchise—bolstered by its scale and "fortress" balance sheet—captures sticky low-cost funding while assets reprice higher. As rate cut bets evaporate, the NIM compression feared by analysts simply fails to materialize at the scale predicted. Meanwhile, fee businesses in investment banking and markets provide the diversification that turns NII stability into earnings power.

Critics whine about "plateauing" margins. Reality: consensus Q1 2026 estimates already baked in stagnation, yet JPMorgan's guidance upgrade signals resilience. With the Fed's March dot plot still nodding to just one cut (widely doubted by markets), and JPM's internal view calling for none, the bank benefits from a yield curve that refuses to invert or collapse. Loan demand holds because the economy doesn't need cheap money to function—corporates and consumers adapted to 4%+ rates years ago.

This setup exposes the fragility of rate-cut bulls. Banks that loaded up on duration bets or assumed endless easing are staring at squeezed spreads. JPMorgan? It never bet the farm on Powell pivoting. Its outlook assumes balance sheet growth offsets any minor pressure, delivering steady income that funds buybacks, dividends, and AI-driven efficiency plays.

The brutal math: $104.5 billion in NII against $105 billion expenses leaves operating leverage intact even as the bank pours capital into growth areas. That's not the end of the windfall era—it's the maturation of a model that prints cash regardless of the Fed's next move. Rate cuts priced out means the easy money trade is dead, but the real money machine at JPMorgan just shifted into a higher gear.

verdict

JPMorgan's upgraded NII outlook shreds the rate-cut euphoria gripping Wall Street. In a world where the Fed holds steady at 3.5-3.75% and yields cling near 4.3%, this banking behemoth delivers fortress-level income without apology. Forget normalization narratives—the data shows sustained profitability, disciplined costs, and a business model engineered for exactly this environment. Buy the resilience, ignore the doves. Higher rates aren't killing banks; they're exposing the weak ones while rewarding the prepared.
