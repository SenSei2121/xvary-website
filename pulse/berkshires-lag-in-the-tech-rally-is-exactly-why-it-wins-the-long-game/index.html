Berkshire's lag in the tech rally is exactly why it wins the long game

Wall Street calls it weakness. The cash pile and earnings machine say otherwise.

xvary research desk · april 15, 2026 · ~3 min read

You hear it on every screen: Berkshire Hathaway shares are getting left behind. While the S&P 500 charges to fresh records on narrow tech leadership, BRK.B sits down 5.5% year-to-date through mid-April 2026 versus the index up roughly 4%. CNBC and the crowd frame it as post-Buffett drift, proof the conglomerate missed the AI-fueled party and lacks growth exposure. Fair surface read. But the real story flips hard when you look at the balance sheet and operating core. That deliberate lag isn't weakness—it's the repeatable edge that has let Berkshire deliver superior risk-adjusted compounding across full market cycles for decades.

The market is all-in on concentrated growth names at premium multiples. Berkshire is doing the opposite: sitting on $373.3 billion in cash and short-term investments at the end of 2025, according to its 2025 annual report and 10-K—roughly one-third of its market cap. That war chest was built through disciplined net equity trimming into strength, not panic selling. Per the same filings, Berkshire generated $46 billion in net cash from operating activities in 2025, well above the five-year average of more than $40 billion. Meanwhile, the S&P 500's blended P/E sits in the mid-20s while BRK.B trades at a trailing P/E of 15.3x as of mid-April 2026 data. You get paid to wait with a fortress that most CEOs can only dream about.

That cash isn't dead weight—it's strategic dry powder earned at low risk while the index chases high-beta concentration. Look at the businesses underneath. Full-year 2025 operating earnings hit $44.5 billion across insurance, BNSF rail, and energy operations, per Berkshire's February 2026 earnings release and annual report—below 2024's $47.4 billion but still comfortably above the $37.5 billion five-year average. These aren't sexy, but they throw off reliable capital regardless of whether Big Tech rips or retrenches. The $373.3 billion liquidity gives Greg Abel massive optionality: pounce on dislocations when prices reset, or simply earn a low-risk spread on Treasuries while everyone else rides beta. The setup blunts downside and loads upside for when valuations decompress.

Here's the variant perception the street glosses over. Consensus obsesses over the short-term gap—BRK.B trailing the S&P by 9-10 points in recent stretches—as evidence of irrelevance in a growth-obsessed world. Reality is the punchline: this pattern has repeated for years. Berkshire's diversified, low-beta machine and liquidity buffer position it to outperform precisely when narrow leadership falters. The S&P carries elevated concentration and valuation risk; Berkshire carries deployment optionality. History confirms it—Berkshire routinely lags in frothy rallies driven by a handful of names, then pulls ahead as the cycle turns and capital gets redeployed at better prices.

Deadpan fact bomb: Berkshire ended 2025 with $373.3 billion in cash—more than the market cap of all but a handful of companies—while the S&P 500's Shiller CAPE ratio hovered near 37-39 in early 2026, levels last seen near major tops. You don't amass that kind of war chest by chasing momentum. You build it by ignoring the noise and letting the businesses compound quietly.

Screenshottable stat line: BRK.B -5.5% YTD (mid-April 2026) vs. S&P 500 +4%; $373.3B cash & short-term investments end-2025 (~1/3 market cap, per 2025 10-K/annual report); operating earnings $44.5B FY2025 (above $37.5B 5-year avg); trailing P/E 15.3x vs. S&P blended mid-20s.

So what would actually kill the thesis? Measurable breaks that falsify the structural edge. If the S&P 500 corrects more than 20% from current levels with no meaningful Berkshire cash deployment or operating earnings growth by end-Q3 2026. Or if Q2 2026 earnings show operating profit declining over 10% year-over-year with insurance underwriting margins cracking below historical averages. Cash falling below $300 billion by October 2026 without offsetting equity portfolio gains or acquisitions yielding above 8% ROE would signal trouble. Same for any clear pivot from value discipline under Abel—oversized acquisitions at stretched prices or aggressive buybacks well above 1.5x book value.

The numbers don't lie. Berkshire isn't lagging because it's broken; it's lagging because it's built different—patient capital allocator with a cash-generative core the market currently discounts. The narrow rally's winners look unstoppable today. Full-cycle history says the disciplined compounder with dry powder wins when the music stops and reality delivers the punchline.
