The Market Calls Berkshire’s Lag Weakness. The Cash Pile Says Otherwise.

While the S&P races on tech fumes, Berkshire sits on record dry powder and a discount that screams preparation—not procrastination.

xvary research desk · april 13, 2026 · ~3 min read

You’ve seen the headlines: Berkshire Hathaway shares are getting left behind again. As the S&P 500 charges to new records on a handful of high-beta tech names, BRK.B has dropped about 5.5% year-to-date through mid-April 2026 while the index sits up roughly 4%. That gap blew out to nearly 10 points in recent weeks. CNBC calls it post-Buffett drift. The street whispers missed AI exposure and too much caution. Here’s the sharper truth: this lag isn’t a bug in Berkshire’s machine—it’s the feature that has delivered superior long-term compounding every time concentration cracks.

Start with the balance sheet that most analysts treat as boring. Berkshire ended 2025 with $373.3 billion in cash and short-term investments—roughly one-third of its market cap, per the year-end 10-K. That’s not idle money sitting idle; it’s low-risk liquidity built while the S&P pushed valuations into dangerous territory. Greg Abel doesn’t need to chase every rally. He waits for the reset that always comes when narrow leadership dominates. You own real optionality here, not narrative risk.

Underneath that cash fortress runs a cash-generative operating engine the market keeps undervaluing. Full-year 2025 operating earnings reached $44.5 billion, powered by stable insurance float, BNSF rail traffic, and energy assets—even as Q4 came in at $10.2 billion amid some underwriting noise. That’s $46 billion in operating cash flow for the year, funding buybacks, small tuck-ins, and the occasional big swing without dilution or distress sales. While growth chasers swing for the fences and pray sentiment holds, Berkshire grinds out durable earnings that survive corrections.

Valuation sharpens the contrast. BRK.B trades at a trailing P/E of around 15.3x versus the S&P 500’s blended 24-25x multiple. Price-to-book hovers near 1.48x—right where history shows disciplined capital allocation (buybacks and acquisitions) historically accelerates. Add in Berkshire’s low beta versus the S&P’s concentration-driven volatility, and you see two different animals: one riding a narrow, high-multiple bet; the other a diversified, cash-rich compounder priced for skepticism. The street obsesses over short-term relative performance. It ignores how this setup wins when the music stops.

History isn’t subtle on this pattern. Berkshire routinely trails in rallies fueled by a few winners, then pulls ahead over full cycles as concentration unwinds and dry powder gets deployed at better prices. The variant perception is clear: the market treats the lag as proof of irrelevance in a “new paradigm.” Reality says it’s structural discipline. While the S&P’s CAPE ratio sits near 36-39—levels last seen before major tops—Berkshire’s cash hoard exceeds the market cap of most individual companies. That mismatch doesn’t signal weakness. It signals the next buying opportunity is being handed to patient owners on a silver platter.

Deadpan fact bomb: Berkshire closed 2025 with more cash than the market cap of all but a handful of companies on the planet, while the S&P 500 traded at a CAPE near levels that preceded some of the worst drawdowns in modern markets. The lag you’re seeing isn’t drift—it’s the calm before the capital redeployment that has rewarded Berkshire shareholders for decades.

So the picture holds unless specific triggers break the setup. If the S&P 500 corrects more than 20% from current levels by end-Q3 2026 without Berkshire showing meaningful cash deployment or operating earnings growth, the thesis takes a hit. Same if Q2 2026 operating earnings fall over 10% year-over-year with insurance underwriting margins cracking below historical averages. Or if the cash position slides below $300 billion by October 2026 without offsetting equity gains or acquisitions clearing an 8% ROE bar. And if Greg Abel signals a clear break from value discipline—chasing large overpriced deals or hammering buybacks well above 1.5x book—that’s your cue to reassess. Until those lines are crossed, the setup remains intact.

Bottom line: stop treating Berkshire’s lag like a problem to solve. It’s the exact edge you want when the current rally’s narrow foundation finally gives. The cash is real, the earnings are durable, the valuation is cheap on a risk-adjusted basis. Accumulate the lag before the reset forces the crowd to chase the same dry powder at higher prices. Reality, as always, is the punchline.
