Executive Summary

Iran has closed or severely restricted the Strait of Hormuz since early March 2026 amid the ongoing US-Israeli military conflict that began in late February, disrupting ~20% of global seaborne oil and LNG trade. This has triggered the largest supply shock in history with over 10 million barrels per day of production shortfalls, driving crude prices to $100-130/bbl and sparking immediate demand destruction signals. A two-week ceasefire took effect in early April with fragile diplomatic talks continuing, while US naval measures enforce the blockade on Iranian-linked vessels.

First-order market moves include sharp rallies in energy equities (XOM, CVX, COP, OXY all +4 magnitude) and oil services (SLB, HAL), offset by heavy pressure on airlines (DAL, UAL -4) and broader industrials/consumer discretionary sectors from elevated fuel costs.

Second- and third-order effects include fertilizer price surges benefiting MOS (+3) via disrupted seaborne trade, gold and USD strength as safe havens, and potential knock-on inflation pass-through into materials and food prices. Watch for cascading demand destruction in travel and manufacturing, plus knock-on LNG shortages amplifying European energy volatility.

Relevant analogues are the 2020 Saudi-Russia oil price war and the 2022 Russian gas cutoff to Europe, though both break down here due to the military chokepoint nature versus pure production/pricing disputes. The event remains novel in scale for a physical strait closure.

Key uncertainties center on ceasefire durability, Iranian proxy responses, and exact enforcement of the US naval blockade. A full reopening or sustained military escalation would materially alter the duration and magnitude of the shock.

The PM must monitor developments daily as any breakthrough in talks or escalation can shift prices 10-20% intraday.

The ongoing 2026 US-Israeli military conflict with Iran, which began in late February, has led to Iran effectively closing or severely restricting traffic through the Strait of Hormuz since early March. This chokepoint normally carries about 20% of global seaborne oil and LNG trade. The resulting supply disruption—described by the IEA as the largest in history—has caused oil production shortfalls of over 10 million barrels per day, price spikes above $100-130 per barrel, and demand destruction forecasts. A temporary two-week ceasefire was agreed in early April with ongoing diplomatic talks (including failed Islamabad round and potential resumption), amid US naval blockade measures targeting Iranian-linked shipping.
