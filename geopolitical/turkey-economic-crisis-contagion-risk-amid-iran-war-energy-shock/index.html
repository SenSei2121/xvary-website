Executive Summary

The US-Iran conflict triggered closure of the Strait of Hormuz and a US naval blockade, spiking Brent crude from ~$72 to over $119/bbl and disrupting Turkey’s recovery program built on $65/bbl oil assumptions. Turkey’s central bank drew down over $26 billion in reserves—including 56 tons of gold sales—to defend the lira amid a surging energy import bill and $197.6 billion corporate FX short position, widening the current account deficit and pushing USD/TRY higher. Energy sector majors gained immediately: XOM, CVX, and COP each saw upstream revenue tailwinds from the +47/bbl spike. European banks with Turkish exposure—BBVA (mag=3 downside), SAN, ING—faced immediate pressure from FX debt rollover risks and contagion fears.

Second- and third-order effects include accelerated lira depreciation feeding into 30%+ inflation, corporate debt stress in FX-exposed industrials and materials (mag=2-3 downside), and potential EM contagion via European bank balance sheets. Safe-haven flows lifted GLD. Turkish refinery TUPRAS absorbed higher crude costs on near-total import dependence.

Key uncertainties center on Hormuz reopening timeline and duration of the ~12 mbd supply shortfall. PMs must monitor developments daily.
