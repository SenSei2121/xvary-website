Executive Summary

US-Iran conflict triggered a US naval blockade of Iranian ports and partial closure of the Strait of Hormuz, slashing throughput from 20 million bpd to 3.8 million bpd and driving Brent crude from $72 to over $119/bbl within three weeks. Turkey, importing over 90% of its crude and 96% of natural gas, faces a widened current account deficit (projected ~$45bn), rapid reserve drawdowns exceeding $26bn in FX and 56 tons of gold in weeks, and lira depreciation pressure. The IMF cut Turkey's 2026 growth forecast to 3.4% from 4.2% due to higher energy costs. Immediate market moves include sharp spikes in oil/gas prices, USD/TRY and EUR/TRY appreciation, and energy sector gains (XOM, CVX, COP up on upstream revenues) alongside Turkish financials selloff (AKBNK, GARAN, ISCTR down on FX mismatch and loan risks). Second- and third-order effects include corporate FX defaults amplifying banking stress, contagion to European banks with Turkish exposure, higher inflation forcing delayed rate cuts in Europe, and industrial slowdowns hitting Turkish autos and materials (FROTO, SISE, TUPRS). Historical analogues—2022 OPEC+ cut and 2020 Saudi-Russia price war—show similar oil spikes benefited producers (+15-25% sector returns) while import-dependent EMs like Turkey saw 30-40%+ lira weakening; the break here is Turkey's elevated $197.6bn corporate FX short position and depleted reserves, raising 2018-style crisis risks. Key uncertainties center on Hormuz reopening timeline, duration of the US blockade, and whether selective Iranian export deals sustain partial flows. The PM needs to act within days on energy and Turkish exposures.
